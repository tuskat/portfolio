## Issues & Suggestions

### 1. Career summary is generic
> "Senior Frontend Developer with 10+ years of experience building high-quality web products. Master's in IT, strong problem-solving skills, and a proven track record delivering under tight timelines across diverse stacks and teams."

This could describe almost anyone. It has no specificity about *what kind* of products, *what scale*, *what outcomes*. Consider something like:

> "Senior Frontend Developer with 10+ years building consumer-facing web products at scale — from VPN dashboards and security tooling (ExpressVPN, Network Guard) to cross-framework migrations. Strong track record shipping under pressure across Vue, React, Angular, and TypeScript stacks."

### 2. Experience entries have no bullet points / achievements
Every role currently only lists a tech stack. There's no answer to *"what did you actually do or accomplish there?"*. ATS systems and humans both scan for impact. For the top 2 roles at minimum, add 2–3 bullet points, e.g.:
- What did you build?
- What did you improve (perf, UX, process)?
- What was the scale (users, traffic, team size)?

Example for Network Guard:
> - Led frontend architecture across Vue.js and React codebases serving 100k+ users
> - Migrated legacy Angular app to TypeScript, reducing runtime errors by X%
> - Mentored junior developers and established component library standards

### 3. Gap between Jul 2016 and Sep 2016 (NLS)
Intralogue ends Feb–Jul 2016, NLS starts Sep 2016. Small gap but unexplained. Not a problem, but be ready to speak to it if asked.

### 4. "Network Guard" is not a well-known company
With 7.5 years there, this is the centrepiece of the CV. Consider adding a one-liner descriptor:
> *Network Guard — cybersecurity / VPN solutions provider*

Same applies to Intralogue, NLS, Haupria — most readers won't know what they do.

### 5. "School of Life, Earth (1991–Present)" in Education
Cute, but risky. Some hiring managers or HR screeners may not appreciate the humour in a professional document, especially in Spain/EU corporate contexts. Keep it if you're targeting startup/creative roles; remove it for enterprise roles.

### 6. "Learning-Sponge" as a personal skill label
Minor: this reads slightly informal/quirky. "Fast Learner" or "Adaptability" would land more neutrally while meaning the same thing. Entirely your call — it may resonate in the right culture.

### 7. Recommender "N IL"
The third recommender's name appears to be truncated or anonymised. This looks odd. Either use the full name or omit the entry. An unnamed recommendation carries very little weight.

### 8. Skills list includes Ruby on Rails, .NET/C#/Unity, Flutter
These appear alongside your core JS/TS stack without any context. If you have real experience with these, great — but if they're incidental, recruiters may ask about them and it could hurt. Consider separating into "Primary" and "Familiar with" groups, or only listing things you're comfortable being tested on.

### 9. LinkedIn is missing
No LinkedIn profile in the contact section. This is the first thing most recruiters check. If you have one, add it.

### 10. Title says "Senior Web Developer" but the body says "Senior Frontend Developer"
Pick one and be consistent. "Senior Frontend Developer" is more specific and searchable.
